# DPDP compliance for an AI astrology app storing birth date-time-place, transcripts, and payment data

**Researched:** 2026-07-17 · **Ticket:** [011-dpdp-research](../wayfinder/engineering/tickets/011-dpdp-research.md) · **Feeds:** [008-data-model-dpdp](../wayfinder/engineering/tickets/008-data-model-dpdp.md)

---

## Executive summary

1. **The regime is real but the heavy obligations are not yet in force.** The DPDP Act 2023 exists; the DPDP Rules 2025 were notified 13–14 November 2025 with a **three-phase commencement**: Board provisions immediately (Nov 2025), Consent Manager registration from **14 Nov 2026**, and **all substantive obligations (notice, consent, security, breach, erasure, rights) from ~13–14 May 2027** ([PIB official notification](https://static.pib.gov.in/WriteReadData/specificdocs/documents/2025/nov/doc20251117695301.pdf), [Shardul Amarchand Mangaldas](https://www.amsshardul.com/insight/enforcement-of-the-dpdp-act-and-notification-of-the-dpdp-rules/)). A concierge MVP launching in 2026 has a ~10-month runway before hard enforcement — but should build to the Rules now, because retrofitting consent/erasure into a data model is far costlier than designing for it. [FACT]
2. **Birth date-time-place is NOT "sensitive personal data" under DPDP — because DPDP abolished the category entirely.** All digital personal data gets one uniform (and fairly strict) consent-first regime. Until May 2027, the old SPDI Rules 2011 sensitive-data tiering technically still applies — and birth data/astrology data was never in the SPDI sensitive list anyway. **Ticket 008's premise ("birth profile — sensitive personal data under DPDP") is legally wrong; the privacy-forward posture should be framed as product positioning, not statutory obligation.** [FACT]
3. **Consent is the workhorse.** Notice must be itemised, purpose-specific, standalone, and available in **English or any of the 22 Eighth-Schedule languages — Malayalam qualifies**; consent must be a clear affirmative act, and **withdrawal must be as easy as giving it**. [FACT]
4. **Cross-border hosting is fine today.** India uses a **blacklist** model: transfer anywhere except countries the Central Government notifies — and **no country has been blacklisted as of mid-2026**. SDF-specific localization exists but won't touch a 50-user MVP. [FACT]
5. **Erasure vs transcript retention resolves via anonymization.** DPDP applies only to data "about an individual"; de-identified/anonymized transcripts fall outside it — but the Act doesn't define an anonymization standard, so treat this as an [ANALYSIS] position and do it properly (strip identifiers AND quasi-identifiers like exact birth data).
6. **Breach duties are stricter than GDPR on paper:** notify **every** affected user *and* the Board "without delay" on becoming aware, plus a detailed Board report within **72 hours** — with **no materiality threshold**. (In force May 2027.) [FACT]
7. **A 20–50-user MVP is nowhere near Significant Data Fiduciary territory** — SDF status requires a specific Central Government notification, and none had issued as of mid-2026. [FACT]
8. **Diaspora users are governed by *their* jurisdictions, not DPDP** — DPDP protects data principals *within India*. UK/EU users can pull the app under UK GDPR/GDPR if the app *targets* them (it will, per the product definition), which brings the stricter special-category question back: **GDPR Art. 9 "religious or philosophical beliefs" plausibly covers astrological consultation data** — this is the single biggest legal-regime asymmetry for astrochat. UAE PDPL is now enforced (executive regulations issued 2026) and also claims extraterritorial reach. [ANALYSIS]
9. **Minimum-viable compliance for the MVP:** Malayalam+English itemised consent screen; one-tap consent withdrawal; delete-my-account that actually cascades; encryption at rest and in transit with access logging; India-hosted or at least India-transferable architecture kept optional; separate consent capture rows per purpose; no raw card storage (leave that to the payment gateway).

## 1. Is birth date-time-place "sensitive" under DPDP?

**No — DPDP has no sensitive/special-category tier at all.**

- [FACT] The DPDP Act adopts a single, uniform definition of "personal data" (s.2(t): "any data about an individual who is identifiable by or in relation to such data") and, unlike the SPDI Rules 2011 (made under s.43A IT Act), does **not** subcategorise sensitive personal data. See [S&R Associates, "Sense and Sensitivity"](https://www.snrlaw.in/sense-and-sensitivity-sensitive-information-under-indias-new-data-regime/) and [Clyde & Co](https://www.clydeco.com/en/insights/2025/12/decrypting-india-s-data-protection-regime-principl).
- [FACT] The SPDI Rules 2011 remain in force **until 13 May 2027**, when s.43A IT Act and the SPDI Rules are repealed alongside full DPDP commencement ([IndusLaw FAQs](https://cms-induslaw.com/en/ind/publication/faqs-on-the-digital-personal-data-protection-act-and-rules), [Opsio](https://opsiocloud.com/in/knowledge-base/are-spdi-rules-still-in-force/)). Even under SPDI, the "sensitive" list (passwords, financial info, health, sexual orientation, medical records, biometrics) **never included birth details or religious/astrological data** — so birth data is ordinary personal data under both the old and new regimes. [ANALYSIS]
- **Implication:** the DPDP compliance bar for birth date-time-place is the *same* as for a name or email: valid consent, purpose limitation, security safeguards, erasure. The elevated treatment astrochat gives birth data is a **product/trust decision** (and a GDPR hedge for diaspora — see §8), not an Indian statutory mandate. [ANALYSIS]
- **Nuance that does raise stakes:** "sensitivity" reappears in DPDP in two places — (a) as a factor for the government designating **Significant Data Fiduciaries** (s.10(1) considers "volume and sensitivity of personal data processed"), and (b) Rule 6 expects safeguards proportionate to data sensitivity. So sensitivity matters for proportionality, not for a separate consent tier. [FACT] ([dpdpa.com Rule 6](https://www.dpdpa.com/dpdparules/rule6.html))
- **Third-party birth data (spouse/child charts):** if a user enters someone *else's* birth details, astrochat is the Data Fiduciary for that third party's personal data, and consent from the *user* is not consent from the *data subject*. For **children under 18**, s.9 requires **verifiable parental/guardian consent** and bans tracking, behavioural monitoring and targeted advertising directed at children ([FPF explainer](https://fpf.org/blog/the-digital-personal-data-protection-act-of-india-explained/)). A child's jathakam (birth chart) request is squarely a s.9 event. [FACT for the s.9 rule; ANALYSIS for its application to relative-chart flows]

## 2. Consent requirements

- [FACT] **Basis:** processing requires consent (s.6) or a "legitimate use" (s.7 — voluntary provision for a specified purpose, state functions, emergencies, employment, etc.). For a consumer astrology app, consent is the operative basis; s.7(a) "voluntary provision" arguably covers data the user types mid-chat for the purpose they asked about, but don't lean on it for stored profiles. [ANALYSIS]
- [FACT] **Quality of consent (s.6(1)):** free, specific, informed, unconditional, unambiguous, given by **clear affirmative action**, limited to the specified purpose, and limited to data **necessary** for that purpose. No bundling, no pre-ticked boxes.
- [FACT] **Notice (s.5 + Rule 3):** must accompany or precede the consent request; must be **understandable independently** (standalone, not buried in a ToS); must contain an **itemised description of the personal data** collected, the **specified purpose(s)**, the goods/services enabled, and the means to (i) withdraw consent, (ii) exercise data-principal rights, (iii) complain to the Data Protection Board. ([ApniLaw s.5 text](https://www.apnilaw.com/bare-act/dpdp/section-5-digital-personal-data-protection-act-dpdp-notice/), [Seclore Rules guide](https://www.seclore.com/fundamentals/dpdp-rules-2025-compliance-guide/))
- [FACT] **Language (s.6(3)):** the data principal must have "the option to access" the consent request **in English or any of the 22 languages in the Eighth Schedule** — Malayalam is one of the 22 ([Certinal on multilingual consent](https://www.certinal.com/blog/multilingual-consent-under-dpdp-act)). For a Malayali user base this means a Malayalam consent notice is effectively mandatory (the user gets to choose the language, and yours will choose Malayalam). [ANALYSIS on the practical framing]
- [FACT] **Withdrawal (s.6(4)–(6)):** must be **as easy as giving consent** (one-tap in ⇒ one-tap out); withdrawal doesn't invalidate prior processing; on withdrawal the fiduciary must **cease processing and cause its processors to cease** within a reasonable time, and erase unless retention is legally required (s.8(7)).
- [FACT] **Consent Managers:** a registered-intermediary framework letting users manage consents across fiduciaries via one interoperable platform. Registration with the Board opens **14 November 2026** ([SAM](https://www.amsshardul.com/insight/enforcement-of-the-dpdp-act-and-notification-of-the-dpdp-rules/)). Nothing obliges a small fiduciary to integrate with Consent Managers at launch; watch the ecosystem post-Nov-2026. [ANALYSIS]
- [FACT] **Rights plumbing (Rule 14):** fiduciary must publish the means and identifiers by which users exercise rights, and answer grievances "within a reasonable period **not exceeding ninety days**" (Rule 14(3)) ([dpdpa.com Rule 14](https://www.dpdpa.com/dpdparules/rule14.html)).

## 3. Data residency / cross-border transfers

- [FACT] **Blacklist model (s.16 + Rule 15):** "A Data Fiduciary may transfer personal data outside India except where the Central Government restricts such transfer." This is a negative list — the opposite of GDPR adequacy ([dpdpa.com Rule 15](https://www.dpdpa.com/dpdparules/rule15.html), [Medianama](https://www.medianama.com/2025/11/223-dpdp-rules-cross-border-data-transfers/), [K&K analysis](https://ksandk.com/data-protection-and-data-privacy/indias-new-cross-border-data-transfer-framework/)).
- [FACT] **No restricted country has been notified as of mid-2026** ([DSCI FAQs on cross-border flow](https://www.dsci.in/files/content/documents/2025/cross-border-flow-of-data.pdf)). The provision itself only bites from May 2027.
- [FACT] **SDF-only localization:** Significant Data Fiduciaries may be barred from transferring **specified categories** of personal data/traffic data outside India, as identified by a government committee (Rule 13 territory) ([SAM](https://www.amsshardul.com/insight/enforcement-of-the-dpdp-act-and-notification-of-the-dpdp-rules/)). Irrelevant at MVP scale, relevant to long-term infra strategy. [ANALYSIS]
- [FACT] **Rule 15 caveat:** transfers to a foreign "State or its agencies" may be subject to conditions the Central Government specifies — in practice this is read as a constraint around foreign-government access demands to data held abroad. [ANALYSIS] ([ELP note](https://elplaw.in/wp-content/uploads/2025/12/Cross-border-data-transfers-under-the-DPDP-Act-what-businesses-need-to-know.pdf))
- **Practical read for astrochat:** hosting on any mainstream cloud region (India, Singapore, US, EU) is lawful under DPDP today and almost certainly post-May-2027 for non-SDFs. **However**: payment flows have a *separate, sectoral* localization — RBI's 2018 directive requires **payment-system data to be stored only in India**, which binds your payment gateway (Razorpay/PhonePe/etc.), not you, so long as you never store card data yourself. Choosing an India region anyway is cheap insurance + latency win for Kerala users. [ANALYSIS]

## 4. Erasure & retention vs keeping transcripts for AI eval

- [FACT] **Right to erasure (s.12 + s.8(7)):** users may demand erasure; independent of requests, the fiduciary must erase (and cause processors to erase) when consent is withdrawn **or** the specified purpose is no longer being served — unless retention is required by law (tax, accounting, CERT-In log mandates, etc.).
- [FACT] **Rule 8 + Third Schedule "use it or lose it":** default retention windows (3 years from last interaction) apply only to **specified large classes** — e-commerce and social-media intermediaries with ≥2 crore users, online gaming with ≥50 lakh — with a mandatory **48-hour advance notice** to the user before time-based erasure ([dpdpa.com Rule 8](https://www.dpdpa.com/dpdparules/rule8.html), [K&K retention note](https://ksandk.com/data-protection-and-data-privacy/data-retention-and-deletion-under-indias-dpdp-rules/)). astrochat is in none of those classes at MVP scale; the general s.8(7) purpose-limitation rule is what governs. [FACT + ANALYSIS on applicability]
- **Anonymization as the out:** [ANALYSIS] DPDP applies only to *personal* data ("about an individual who is identifiable by or in relation to such data", s.2(t)). Data genuinely stripped of identifiability falls outside the Act — but **the Act and Rules define no anonymization/de-identification standard**, so this is an interpretive position, not black-letter safe harbour ([Linklaters Data Protected — India](https://www.linklaters.com/en/insights/data-protected/data-protected---india)). Two consequences:
  1. Do it rigorously: for astrology transcripts, the **exact birth date-time-place is itself a quasi-identifier** (a birth timestamp + village narrows to ~1 person). Anonymized eval corpora must generalize or perturb birth data (e.g., keep lagna/nakshatra-level derived features, drop the raw timestamp/place), strip names, phone numbers, and free-text identity leakage from the chat.
  2. Be honest in the notice: state that de-identified excerpts may be retained for service improvement even after account deletion. Consent covers the personal-data phase; anonymization covers the after-life. [ANALYSIS]
- **Do NOT rely on "consent to keep transcripts forever":** consent is purpose-bound and withdrawable; a withdrawal collapses the retention basis for identifiable transcripts (s.6(6), s.8(7)). Design the pipeline so eval value survives erasure: anonymize continuously, not at deletion time. [ANALYSIS]

## 5. Breach notification

- [FACT] **Two-tier duty (s.8(6) + Rule 7)**, in force from May 2027 ([dpdpa.com Rule 7](https://www.dpdpa.com/dpdparules/rule7.html), [Rule 7 guide](https://www.cybernx.com/data-breach-notification-under-dpdpa/)):
  - **Without delay** on becoming aware: notify **the Board AND every affected Data Principal** — concise, plain-language description of the breach's nature/extent/timing, likely consequences for the user, mitigation measures, safety steps the user can take, and a contact for queries.
  - **Within 72 hours** (extendable on written request): detailed report to the Board — facts and causes, mitigation, findings on who caused it, remedial steps, and a summary of the user notifications sent.
- [FACT] **No materiality/risk threshold** — unlike GDPR (which notifies individuals only on "high risk"), DPDP requires notifying users for **every** personal-data breach ([Consently guide](https://www.consently.in/blog/dpdp-72-hour-breach-notification-rule-india-guide)). "Breach" is defined broadly (s.2(u): unauthorized processing **or accidental disclosure, use, alteration, loss of access, or destruction**). [FACT]
- [FACT] **Penalty exposure:** failure of security safeguards → up to **₹250 crore**; failure to notify Board/users of a breach → up to **₹200 crore** (Act, Schedule) ([iSpectra penalty table](https://ispectratechnologies.com/hub/dpdp/dpdp-penalties.html)).
- **Product implication:** you need a breach runbook and the technical ability to enumerate affected users and message them (email/app notification) — trivial at 50 users, but the transcript store makes "accidental disclosure" scenarios (e.g., a transcript shown to the wrong account) a *reportable breach*, so session isolation bugs are compliance events, not just UX bugs. [ANALYSIS]

## 6. Significant Data Fiduciary — does the MVP clear the bar?

**No.**

- [FACT] SDF status exists **only by Central Government notification** (s.10(1)), assessed on: volume & sensitivity of data, risk to data principals' rights, potential impact on sovereignty & integrity of India, risk to electoral democracy, security of the State, and public order.
- [FACT] **No SDF notifications had issued as of mid-2026**; SDF designations remain pending ([CADP implementation tracker](https://cadp.in/resources/guides/dpdp-implementation-tracker/)). Circulating numeric thresholds (e.g., "50 lakh data principals" / "₹250 crore revenue") are **consultant speculation, not notified law** — see the unverified list. [ANALYSIS]
- [FACT] If ever designated: appoint an India-resident **DPO** reporting to the board of the company, an independent data auditor, **annual DPIA + annual audit** (Rule 13), algorithmic due-diligence duty, and possible data-localization orders for specified data classes ([dpdpa.com Rule 13](https://www.dpdpa.com/dpdparules/rule13.html), [SAM](https://www.amsshardul.com/insight/enforcement-of-the-dpdp-act-and-notification-of-the-dpdp-rules/)).
- **Triggers to watch later:** [ANALYSIS] (a) scale — millions of Indian users; (b) the *sensitivity* factor — a government could plausibly view intimate life-consultation transcripts (marriage, health anxieties, finances) + birth data as high-sensitivity even without a statutory tier; (c) profiling/AI-driven decisions affecting users. None of this is a 2026–27 concern at concierge scale, but note it in the scaling plan.
- [FACT] Even non-SDFs carry the full baseline: consent, notice, security safeguards, breach notification, erasure, grievance redressal ≤90 days. The MVP-relevant relief is only from DPO/DPIA/audit/localization. (There's also a Rule-based exemption power for startups/small fiduciaries from certain notice/accuracy provisions under s.17(3) — government may notify classes; check whether any such notification benefits you at launch.) [ANALYSIS]

## 8. Diaspora users: GDPR / UK GDPR / Gulf

**DPDP does not protect your diaspora users.** [FACT] s.3 applies to (a) digital personal data processed **within India** and (b) processing **outside India** "in connection with any activity related to offering of goods or services to Data Principals **within the territory of India**" ([FPF](https://fpf.org/blog/the-digital-personal-data-protection-act-of-india-explained/), [Fox Mandal](https://foxmandal.in/indias-new-digital-privacy-law-the-dpdp-act-and-rules-explained/)). A Malayali in Dubai or London is not a data principal within India; their protection comes from their local law. (s.3 also excludes personal data made publicly available by the data principal, and purely personal/domestic processing.) [FACT]

**UK/EU (UK GDPR & GDPR):** [ANALYSIS unless noted]
- Art. 3(2) targeting test [FACT as to the rule]: a non-EU/UK controller is caught if it **offers goods/services to** people in the EU/UK. Inviting UK-resident users, taking GBP payments, or marketing to UK Malayali associations = targeting. At 20–50 invited users including UK residents, assume UK GDPR applies to those users.
- **The special-category problem:** GDPR Art. 9 prohibits processing data revealing **"religious or philosophical beliefs"** without explicit consent. A user's engagement with jyothisham consultation — and arguably the consultation content itself — plausibly *reveals* religious/philosophical belief. Health, marriage, and family topics surfacing in transcripts add more Art. 9 surface (health data). **Mitigation: obtain explicit, granular consent (Art. 9(2)(a)) at signup for UK/EU users** — which, conveniently, is roughly what DPDP demands anyway. This is the key asymmetry: *under DPDP birth data is ordinary; under GDPR the consultation context likely makes it special-category.*
- Other UK/EU deltas: lawful-basis records, privacy notice per Arts. 13/14, DSARs in 1 month (vs DPDP's 90 days), breach: 72h to ICO only if risk, users only if high risk; **Art. 27 EU/UK representative** requirement for non-established controllers (small-scale/occasional processing exemption in Art. 27(2)(a) is unlikely to stretch to systematic special-category processing — flag to counsel). International transfer rules run the *other* direction too: UK→India transfers need a transfer mechanism (IDTA/SCCs), since India has no UK/EU adequacy decision.
- Practically, at concierge scale: a combined DPDP+GDPR-grade consent flow, a real deletion path, and an appointed EU/UK contact address cover the material risk. Full GDPR programme work (RoPA, DPIA) can be lightweight at 50 users but should exist on paper.

**Gulf (UAE focus, most Kerala-diaspora-relevant):** [ANALYSIS unless noted]
- [FACT] UAE PDPL (Federal Decree-Law 45/2021) applies extraterritorially to processing of data of **data subjects residing in the UAE** by controllers outside the UAE; its **Executive Regulations were issued and enforcement is active as of 2026**, fines up to AED 5M ([Chambers UAE 2026](https://practiceguides.chambers.com/practice-guides/data-protection-privacy-2026/uae/trends-and-developments), [official law text](https://uaelegislation.gov.ae/en/legislations/1972/download)).
- UAE PDPL retains a **"sensitive data"** category that includes data revealing **religion** and health — the same Art. 9-style exposure as GDPR. Explicit consent is the pragmatic answer again. Saudi Arabia's PDPL (enforced since Sept 2024) similarly claims extraterritorial scope and treats religious data as sensitive; same mitigation. Free-zone regimes (DIFC/ADGM) don't apply to a consumer app.
- **US:** no federal comprehensive law; state laws (CCPA/CPRA, etc.) have applicability thresholds (revenue / 100k+ consumers) a concierge MVP does not meet. Practical US obligation ≈ honest privacy policy + honoring deletion requests. [ANALYSIS]

## 9. Concrete product implications

### The consent screen (DPDP s.5/s.6 + Rule 3, GDPR Art. 9 hardened)
Must be standalone (not linked ToS prose), in **Malayalam and English** (user-selectable), and contain:
1. **Itemised data list:** name/contact; birth date, time, place; consultation messages (voice/text) and transcripts; payment/transaction records (via gateway — no card numbers held); device/technical data if collected.
2. **Specified purposes, separately consented where separable:** (a) generating your chart & providing consultations; (b) service improvement using de-identified data; (c) communications. Purpose (b) should be a distinct toggle — bundling it into (a) fails "specific/unconditional." [ANALYSIS]
3. **Explicit acknowledgment for UK/EU/Gulf users** that consultation data may reveal religious/philosophical beliefs and they explicitly consent to its processing (Art. 9(2)(a) / UAE sensitive-data consent).
4. **Mechanics:** how to withdraw (one tap, same place), how to exercise access/correction/erasure rights, grievance contact + ≤90-day promise, how to complain to the Data Protection Board.
5. **Third-party charts:** an interstitial when entering someone else's birth details — user affirms authority/consent of that person; **block or parental-consent-gate under-18 charts** (s.9). [ANALYSIS on mechanism; FACT on s.9]

### What must be encrypted / secured (Rule 6, from May 2027 but build now)
- [FACT] Rule 6 names **encryption, obfuscation, masking, or virtual tokens**; access control; **access/processing logs retained ≥1 year**; monitoring for unauthorized access; backups for continuity; contractual safeguards flowing to processors ([amlegals](https://amlegals.com/reasonable-security-safeguards-under-dpdpa/), [dpdpa.com Rule 6](https://www.dpdpa.com/dpdparules/rule6.html)).
- Applied: TLS in transit everywhere; at-rest encryption for the DB; **application-layer encryption (or at minimum column-level) for birth data and transcripts** — the two crown jewels; secrets in a KMS; role-based access so the founder-astrologer sees only assigned consultations; audit log on every read of a birth profile or transcript. [ANALYSIS — Rule 6 is outcome-based; this is a proportionate implementation]
- Payments: integrate a gateway (Razorpay/Stripe India), store only gateway tokens/references + a ledger — never PAN/card data (RBI tokenization rules prohibit merchant card storage anyway). [FACT as to RBI rule; check gateway specifics]

### What must be deletable (s.12, s.8(7), Rule 8/14)
- Account + profile (including all birth profiles the user created), consultation transcripts, chart artifacts, contact data — full cascade on erasure request or consent withdrawal, ≤90 days, including **causing processors (LLM API logs? analytics? backups within cycle) to erase**. [FACT as to obligation]
- May survive deletion: (a) data retention **required by law** (payment/tax ledger entries — keep the financial ledger separable from the identity graph); (b) **properly anonymized** transcript derivatives for eval (see §4 — strip raw birth timestamps/places, names, contact info, and free-text identity leakage). [ANALYSIS]

### Minimum-viable compliance checklist (concierge MVP, 2026)
- [ ] Bilingual (ML/EN) standalone consent notice with itemised data + purposes; consent event stored as a **consent record** (who, when, notice version, language, purposes, granular toggles).
- [ ] One-tap consent withdrawal + working account-deletion flow with full cascade and processor propagation.
- [ ] Explicit-consent wording that satisfies GDPR Art. 9 / UAE sensitive-data for diaspora users (one flow can serve all jurisdictions).
- [ ] Child gate (DOB check on the *user*; parental-consent or refusal path for under-18 charts).
- [ ] Third-party-chart authority affirmation interstitial.
- [ ] Encryption at rest + in transit; column/app-layer encryption for birth data & transcripts; access logging (retain 1 yr).
- [ ] Privacy policy published (SPDI Rules 2011 currently require this) + grievance contact + response ≤90 days.
- [ ] Breach runbook: detect → enumerate affected users → notify users & Board without delay → 72-hour Board report. Keep a breach-notification template drafted.
- [ ] No raw payment-instrument storage; gateway tokens only.
- [ ] Continuous anonymization pipeline for the eval corpus (don't anonymize at deletion time).
- [ ] Calendar reminders: **Nov 2026** (Consent Manager ecosystem opens; check startup-exemption notifications), **May 2027** (full DPDP force; SPDI repealed; blacklist countries may be notified).

4. **Anonymization as an escape from DPDP.** Widely-held [ANALYSIS] position with no statutory definition or Board guidance yet; a future Board could adopt a strict re-identification standard. Get counsel sign-off on the eval-corpus design.
5. **GDPR Art. 9 characterization of astrology-consultation data** as "religious or philosophical beliefs" — my analysis by analogy; no ICO/EDPB guidance found squarely on astrology apps. The explicit-consent mitigation is cheap, so do it regardless.
6. **Startup/small-fiduciary exemption notifications under s.17(3)** — whether any class notification exists that benefits a company of astrochat's size at launch; check MeitY notifications.
7. **Rule 7 first-notification content details and the "location need not be disclosed to users" nuance** — drawn from secondary summaries; read Rule 7 text before drafting the breach template.

## Implications for the data model (→ ticket 008)

**First: correct ticket 008's premise.** Birth date-time-place is **not** "sensitive personal data under DPDP" — DPDP has no such category (§1). Keep the elevated handling, but re-justify it as (a) product trust positioning, (b) GDPR/UAE special-category hedging for diaspora, and (c) Rule 6 proportionality. This changes *why*, not *what*.

1. **Consent records are a first-class entity, not a boolean.** Schema: `consent_event(user_id, notice_version, language_shown, purposes[] (service | improvement | comms), granted_at, withdrawn_at, mechanism)`. Immutable, append-only — it's your evidence before the Board (and ICO). AI-disclosure acknowledgment can ride the same table as a distinct purpose/ack type.
2. **Birth profile as a separable, own-table entity** with app/column-layer encryption, per-read audit logging, and a `subject_type` discriminator: `self | third_party_adult | minor`. Third-party rows carry the authority-affirmation reference; minor rows carry parental-consent reference or don't exist (s.9). This directly answers 008's "whether third-party charts are modeled" — yes, model them, with consent metadata on the row.
3. **Transcripts: design for two lifecycles.** (a) Identified transcript, encrypted, linked to user, dies on erasure/withdrawal (≤90 days, cascading to LLM-provider logs — check provider retention/ZDR options). (b) Anonymized eval derivative, produced by a **continuous pipeline** (not at deletion time), with raw birth timestamp/place generalized to derived astrological features, names/contacts stripped. Keep (b) in a store with no FK back to (a).
4. **Chart artifacts (store vs recompute):** compliance nudges toward **recompute-on-demand or cache-with-TTL** — every stored derivative of birth data widens the erasure cascade and breach blast radius. If dasha trees are precomputed for cost, key them to the encrypted birth-profile ID so erasure cascades naturally.
5. **Wallet/ledger stub:** keep **financial records separable from the identity graph** — legal retention duties (tax/accounting) will outlive account erasure; the ledger should survive with the user reference tombstoned/pseudonymized, not the whole account retained.
6. **Erasure as a designed operation:** a `deletion_request(user_id, requested_at, completed_at, scope)` entity plus a documented cascade map (profiles → transcripts → charts → analytics → processor APIs → backups-on-cycle). The 90-day clock and the 48-hour pre-erasure notice pattern (if you later adopt inactivity-based deletion) both need timestamps to hang off.
7. **Access-log table** for reads of birth profiles and transcripts, retained ≥1 year (Rule 6) — cheap now, painful to retrofit.
8. **Residency:** no DPDP requirement to host in India today (§3), but pick an architecture where the primary data store's region is a config decision, not a rewrite — May 2027 blacklist notifications and any future SDF localization then become ops changes. India region recommended anyway for latency + RBI-adjacent tidiness.
